📐 Math Homework Problems

Learn the difference between good and bad AI prompts

💡 Key Tip

Good prompts focus on understanding the process and building skills.
Bad prompts just seek quick answers without learning.

Problem 1
Find the derivative of 3ex · 4x2
Bad Prompt
"What's the derivative of 3e^x * 4x^2?"
Why it's bad: Just asks for answer without learning.
Good Prompt
"I need to find the derivative of 3e^x · 4x^2. Can you explain which differentiation rule I should use and guide me through the steps? I think I need the product rule, but I'm not sure how to apply it with the exponential function."
Why it's good: Shows thinking, asks for process guidance.
Problem 2
Examine the end behavior of (5x3 - 4x4) / (6x5)
Bad Prompt
"What's the end behavior of (5x^3-4x^4)/(6x^5)?"
Why it's bad: Too vague, no understanding shown.
Good Prompt
"I'm analyzing the end behavior of (5x^3-4x^4)/(6x^5). I know I should look at the highest degree terms, but can you walk me through the process step by step? What happens as x approaches positive and negative infinity, and why?"
Why it's good: Shows understanding, seeks reasoning.
Problem 3
We are going to fence in a rectangular field. If we look at the field from above, the cost of the vertical sides are $10/ft, the cost of the bottom is $2/ft, and the cost of the top is $7/ft. If we have $700, determine the dimensions of the field that will maximize the enclosed area.
Bad Prompt
"Solve this optimization problem with a $700 budget and different fence costs."
Why it's bad: No setup or engagement with process.
Good Prompt
"I'm working on an optimization problem. I need to maximize the area of a rectangle with a $700 budget where vertical sides cost $10/ft, bottom costs $2/ft, and top costs $7/ft. Can you help me set up the constraint equation and the area function? Then guide me through using calculus to find the maximum - should I use derivatives and critical points?"
Why it's good: Identifies key elements, asks for method guidance.
Problem 4
Air is being pumped into a spherical balloon at a rate of 5 cm3/min. Determine the rate at which the radius of the balloon is increasing when the diameter of the balloon is 20 cm.
Bad Prompt
"How fast is the radius growing when diameter is 20cm and volume rate is 5 cm^3/min?"
Why it's bad: No strategy or method shown.
Good Prompt
"I'm solving a related rates problem about a spherical balloon. I know dV/dt = 5 cm^3/min and I need to find dr/dt when diameter = 20 cm (radius = 10 cm). Can you help me set up the relationship between volume and radius for a sphere, then guide me through using implicit differentiation with respect to time? What's the key formula I should start with?"
Why it's good: Shows setup, demonstrates understanding, asks for proper method.

🔬 Science Study Methods

Learn how to use AI effectively for science concepts

📋 How to Format a Good AI Prompt

Action: What do you want the AI to do? (Explain, Create, Summarize, etc.)
Audience: Who is this for? (10th grader, beginner, etc.)
Format: How should it be presented? (Flashcards, diagram, simple terms, etc.)

💡 Science Study Tip

Use AI to create different types of study materials for the same concept. This helps reinforce understanding through multiple approaches!

Concept 1
Steps of Photosynthesis
Study Method 1: Flashcard Set
"Create a set of 10 flashcards about the steps of photosynthesis. For each flashcard, put the question on one side (like 'What happens during the light-dependent reactions?') and the answer on the other side. Make them appropriate for a high school biology student and include the key molecules involved."
Why this works: Flashcards help with active recall and are perfect for memorizing sequences.
Study Method 2: Visual Diagram Guide
"Explain the steps of photosynthesis as if I'm creating a visual diagram. Describe what happens in each step, where it occurs in the chloroplast, what molecules are involved, and use simple arrows to show the flow from one step to the next. Use high school biology level language."
Why this works: Visual learning helps understand the process flow and spatial relationships.
Concept 2
Cell Division: Mitosis vs Meiosis
Study Method 1: Comparison Table
"Create a detailed comparison table of mitosis versus meiosis for a high school biology student. Include these categories: number of divisions, number of daughter cells produced, genetic similarity to parent cell, where it occurs in the body, and its biological purpose. Make it easy to study from."
Why this works: Side-by-side comparisons make differences and similarities clear.
Study Method 2: Practice Quiz
"Generate 8 quiz questions that test understanding of the differences between mitosis and meiosis. Mix multiple choice and short answer questions. Include an answer key with brief explanations for each answer. Target high school biology level."
Why this works: Testing yourself reveals knowledge gaps and strengthens memory.
Concept 3
Newton's Laws of Motion
Study Method 1: Real-World Examples
"For each of Newton's 3 laws of motion, provide 3 real-world examples that a high school student can observe in their daily life. Explain clearly how each example demonstrates the specific law. Use simple physics terminology that a beginner would understand."
Why this works: Connecting abstract concepts to everyday experiences improves retention.
Study Method 2: Concept Map Structure
"Help me create a concept map for Newton's Laws of Motion. Describe how to structure it with each main law at the center, then create branches for: key terms and definitions, real-world examples, mathematical formulas, and how the laws relate to each other. Explain the relationships between all these concepts."
Why this works: Concept maps show how ideas connect, helping create a mental framework.
